## Teaching Philosophy: Two Paths to Understanding

**What makes my teaching different?** I believe in two complementary approaches:

### The "Street Fighter" Method ü•ã
- **Physics intuition first**: What's *really* happening?
- **Approximate, estimate, visualize**: Build mental models before diving into formalism
- **Shortcuts and insight**: Use Taylor series, dimensional analysis, physical reasoning
- **Speed and efficiency**: Get to the answer fast, understand the physics deeply

### The "Good Boy" Method üìö
- **Rigorous proofs**: Every step justified, every assumption stated
- **Standard textbook approach**: Formal definitions, theorems, careful logic
- **Mathematical completeness**: Œµ-Œ¥ arguments, proper limit procedures
- **Academic correctness**: What you'd find in Landau & Lifshitz or Rudin

**Most importantly:** I show students *both* approaches side-by-side, so they understand not just *what* the answer is, but *why* it works and *how* to think about it.

## Example: Dual-Method Teaching

Here's a sample from my tutoring work showing both approaches to the same problem:

**Problem**: Show that if $f \in C^2$, $\lim_{x‚Üí‚àû} f(x)$ exists and is finite, and $f''$ is bounded, then $\lim_{x‚Üí‚àû} f'(x) = 0$.

**Street Fighter Approach**: "If $f$ is settling down to a horizontal asymptote, and $f''$ is bounded (so $f'$ can't wiggle too much), then $f'$ *must* go to zero‚Äîotherwise the function would keep rising or falling forever. The bounded second derivative prevents surprises."

**Good Boy Approach**: Construct the sequence $x_n = n\epsilon/M$, apply the Cauchy property to show $|f(x_{n+1}) - f(x_n)| \leq \epsilon^2/M$, use Mean Value Theorem to get $|f'(c_n)| \leq \epsilon$, then bound $|f'(u) - f'(c_n)| \leq M|u - c_n| \leq \epsilon$, concluding $|f'(u)| \leq 2\epsilon$ for arbitrary $\epsilon$.

**Both are correct. Both teach different skills. Students need both.**
